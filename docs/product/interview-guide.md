# Director problem interview guide

## Purpose

This guide is for conversations with community, school, and youth ensemble directors who adapt parts—or avoid repertoire—because their musicians have different skill levels. The interview is exploratory, not a product pitch. Ask about recent, concrete behavior before discussing Particular.

Do not record a session unless the participant has explicitly consented. Keep contact details, recordings, private scores, and raw notes outside this repository in an access-controlled research system.

## Target participants

Recruit directors or arrangers who have, within the last year:

- rewritten or reassigned instrumental parts for mixed-ability players;
- commissioned or asked someone else to adapt parts; or
- rejected repertoire because the available parts were unsuitable for part of the ensemble.

Aim for variation in ensemble type, age group, instrumentation, director experience, and access to arranging support. Avoid treating several staff members from one program as independent evidence of distinct organizational workflows.

## Before the interview

1. Share the research consent summary and answer questions.
2. Confirm the participant is an adult and agrees to take part.
3. Ask whether note-taking is acceptable. Request recording permission separately if recording is useful.
4. Explain that participation does not require sharing a score and that copyrighted or student-related material should not be shared during the interview.
5. Assign a non-identifying participant code. Store the identity-to-code mapping outside git.

## Opening script

> We are exploring how ensemble directors choose and adapt music for groups with mixed playing abilities. I would like to understand what you already do, including what is difficult or works well. This is not a test of you, and I am not looking for a particular answer. Please avoid naming students or sharing confidential or copyrighted files. You may skip any question or stop at any time.

## Core questions

Use follow-ups to obtain a recent example. Do not read every prompt mechanically.

### Ensemble and repertoire

1. Tell me about the ensemble or ensembles you direct: instrumentation, approximate size, rehearsal cadence, and the range of player experience.
2. How do you judge whether a piece is playable by this group?
3. Tell me about the last piece whose parts did not fit the players available.
4. Have you recently decided not to program a piece because of mixed ability? What happened?

### Current adaptation workflow

5. Walk me through the last part you adapted, from recognizing the problem through putting music on the stand.
6. Who did the work, in which tools, and from what source format (notation file, MusicXML, MIDI, PDF, paper, or another source)?
7. Roughly how much hands-on time and elapsed time did it take? What other work was displaced?
8. Which changes did you make, and how did you check that the result still worked with the ensemble?
9. Which musical elements could not be simplified or removed? How did you identify melody, bass, harmony, rhythmic drive, cues, entrances, or exposed material?
10. What varies by instrument when you decide a passage is difficult?
11. What goes wrong most often? How and when do you discover it?
12. Do you create several reusable levels, one-off player-specific parts, optional notes, doubling parts, or another form of differentiation?

### Rights, privacy, and trust

13. What permissions or licensing questions arise when you adapt a score? How do you resolve them today?
14. Which score formats can you legally and practically obtain for typical repertoire?
15. What material would you be unwilling to upload to a third-party service? Why?
16. What evidence would you need before trusting generated parts in rehearsal? What must a director be able to inspect, lock, change, or reverse?
17. How serious would a wrong note, range violation, missing entrance, broken repeat, or layout problem be? Which failures are unacceptable?

### Value and adoption

18. How frequently does this problem occur in a season?
19. What does the current process cost in time, money, repertoire choice, rehearsal time, or player participation?
20. If the work became faster, what would materially improve? What would remain difficult?
21. Who would decide whether to adopt such a tool, and what constraints would affect that decision?
22. How would you evaluate a prototype that generated coordinated Foundation, Core, and Challenge variants from an authorized MusicXML score?

## Optional design-partner discussion

Only after the problem interview, briefly describe Particular and ask:

- Would you review generated output without promising to use it in performance?
- Could you provide structured feedback using a rubric and, if appropriate, join a short follow-up session?
- Might you have a public-domain, original, or explicitly authorized MusicXML score suitable for evaluation?
- Are there organizational approvals, safeguarding rules, licensing limits, or accessibility needs we should plan for?

Interest is not a commitment. Record a design-partner commitment only after the participant receives and accepts the scope in `design-partner-criteria.md` through the private research process.

## Closing

Ask what was missed and whether the participant knows other directors with a meaningfully different workflow. Reconfirm how their information will be used, how to withdraw, and the retention period stated in the consent material.

## Synthesis fields

Transfer only de-identified, aggregated findings into `problem-validation.md`:

- participant code and qualifying segment (kept in the private research system);
- workflow stages and tools;
- frequency and estimated effort ranges;
- avoided repertoire and rehearsal or participation effects;
- input formats and rights constraints;
- instrument- and passage-specific difficulty judgments;
- non-negotiable musical invariants and trust requirements;
- adoption blockers and prototype-review interest;
- contradictory or disconfirming evidence.

Do not commit raw notes or participant-level records to git.
